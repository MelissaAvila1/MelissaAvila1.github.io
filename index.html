Barriers to Education

A discussion and exploration on the issues and consequences surrounding college attainment in the United States.

A Stark Reality
United States Census data shows that while more that 37% of white (non-hispanic) Americans have a college degree, that percentage drops down to 23% for African Americans and only 16.4% for Hispanic Americans. While more young Americans than ever are attaining a college degree, there still exists a stark contrast between different minority and socio-economic groups in the attainment of a formal college education. In the following visualizations we seek to explore the extent of this issue as well as discuss why college remains unattainable for many Americans. This article primarily explores the themes of accessibility, diversity, affordability, and education quality and their effects on graduation and college completion.

The following data comes from the National Center for Education Statistics (NCES) database (citation 1) and the U.S. Department of Education's annual College Scorecard Data (citation 2). As data from the year 2015 has the most complete information, most if not all of the data used in the following visualizations will be from 2015. Data was filtered to include only 4-year private and public institutions with more than a thousand undergratuate students in the United States.
Exploring the Problem
Differences in college attainment and achievement between minoritiy, white, low-income, and high-income students is not a new issue. However, racial and socio-economic background alone only partly account for the complex reasons behind the lack of national college attainment. Rather it is the interaction between these factors as well as other institutionally related aspects like admission rate, faculty salary, endowment, and tuition cost which influence whether young people attend college.

The NCES and College Scorecard data revealed several interesting trends relating to university diversity and completion. The first of these find is how university elitism and exclusivity has resulted in relatively small study body diversity when compared to schools with higher admission rates. The second scatter plot in Figure A shows how university admission rates under 40% have significantly proportions of minorities (and and smaller spread) than universities in the 40% to 80% range. The NY times reports that, even with affirmative action, black and hispanic students are more underrepresented in elite universities than they were 35 years ago (citation 3). While there have been increases in the number of hispanic students attending school, the gap between hispanic students and college-age Hispanic adults has widened since 1980. However, this problem regarding minority acceptance into elite universities stems not simply from issues of equality in admissions consideration, but rather long-standing issues regarding the environments these minority students grow up in (underfunded schools with low quality instruction). Thus, whether the lack of diversity is a result of racial biases in the admissions process or the proportion of college-ready minorities with quality education is still uncertain, but it is likely a culmination of both issues.
